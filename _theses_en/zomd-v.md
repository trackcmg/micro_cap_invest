*Published July 2026. Market cap ~C$52m (~US$38m; ~100.8m shares near C$0.52). Net cash US$22.5m, no bank debt. Reasoning in enterprise value and owner's earnings — cash-box math, not EBITDA multiples.*

## The one-paragraph version

Zoomd runs a performance-marketing platform that acquires paying users for large enterprise advertisers, charging on results across 600+ media sources. It is profitable and genuinely cash-generative: in 2025 it produced US$17.9m of operating cash flow and ended the year with US$22.5m of cash and no long-term debt. The problem, and the opportunity, is that two of its largest customers swapped out a piece of measurement software (their MMP) in late 2025 and paused spend during the transition — an event that has happened only twice in Zoomd's history — which halved quarterly revenue (Q4.25 -50%, Q1.26 -62% year on year) and sent the stock down roughly two-thirds. The market is now pricing near-permanent impairment: with ~US$22.5m of net cash against a ~US$38m market cap, almost 60% of the price is cash, and you are paying only around one times EBITDA and one times cash flow for the entire operating business. That is the asymmetry. If Zoomd merely stays stuck, the cash floor and a still-cash-positive variable-cost model protect the downside. If any one of several independent levers works — the recovering customer returning, 2026 World Cup event revenue, the new E2 sports-betting partnership, the scaling cohort of 2025-onboarded clients, or cheap tuck-in acquisitions bought for their customer books — the business re-rates to a multiple of today's enterprise value.

## What Zoomd does

Zoomd, founded in 2012 and listed on the TSXV since 2019, is a marketing-technology (MarTech) company that sits one layer above the fragmented digital-advertising ecosystem. Its platform connects to 600-700 media sources — the major ad networks plus the long tail of the open internet — and dynamically allocates advertiser budgets to the best-performing channels, presenting a single control centre for user acquisition. The model is performance-based: Zoomd is paid when actual paying users are acquired, not for impressions. It serves large enterprise clients across 12+ verticals — ecommerce, iGaming, FinTech, entertainment — in 50+ countries, and a cornerstone client has historically been Shein, active across many markets.

The structural pitch, in the founder's own framing, is that in-house media-buying teams have become less effective as Apple and Google privacy changes, shifting search feeds and rising complexity make the ecosystem harder to navigate. Because Zoomd connects to hundreds of sources and isn't dependent on any single platform, it can keep delivering results where a single-channel in-house team struggles. It builds population-segment profiles rather than storing personal data (a knowledge-graph approach), which is a genuine advantage in a GDPR world.

## The cash-box math: most of the market cap is cash

Start with the balance sheet, because it is the whole point.

![Cash versus market cap](../../assets/theses/ZOMD.V/cash_vs_marketcap.png)

As of Q1 2026, Zoomd held US$22.5m of cash and no bank debt, against a market capitalization of roughly C$52m (about US$38m). That means net cash is close to 59% of the entire market cap, and the enterprise value — what you are actually paying for the operating business — is only around US$15-16m. For a business that generated US$17.9m of operating cash flow in 2025, that is roughly one times cash flow and about one-and-a-half times EBITDA for the whole company. You are, almost literally, buying the cash and getting the business for a token.

This is the frame that matters. Forget the revenue headline for a moment: the market is valuing Zoomd as though the operating business is worth almost nothing, which is only rational if you believe the earnings power is close to permanently impaired. The rest of this thesis is about why that belief is probably too pessimistic — and why the cash means you are well paid to wait while it is tested.

## What actually happened: the MMP shock

The revenue collapse has a specific, knowable cause, and Chairman Amit Bohensky has explained it directly in interviews and on the earnings calls.

![The two-customer shock](../../assets/theses/ZOMD.V/two_customer_shock.png)

Two of Zoomd's largest customers asked it to halt marketing because they were swapping out their MMP — their Mobile Measurement Partner, the third-party attribution software that tells an advertiser which campaigns are actually driving installs and paying users. When a large advertiser changes its MMP, campaign measurement is disrupted during the migration, and spend is paused until the new attribution stack is validated. Bohensky has said this kind of MMP swap has happened only twice in Zoomd's entire history — it is a genuinely low-probability event, but a high-impact one when it lands on two top customers at once. The two customers were about 63% of revenue at their FY24 peak and 57% in FY25, so pausing both took the top line down hard: Q4.25 revenue fell 50% year on year to US$7.5m, and Q1.26 fell 62% to US$6.9m.

Crucially, this is a process interruption, not a lost account or a competitive defeat. Bohensky's framing on the Q3.25 call was that "such transitions are a part of our business, and based on our experience, activity typically scales back up within a few months." By the FY25 and Q1.26 reports, the picture had split: one of the two customers is "showing early signs of realignment," while visibility with the other "remains limited as the transition continues." So one is coming back and one is still uncertain — which is exactly the kind of ambiguity that creates mispricing, because the market has largely written off both.

I'll be honest about the bear counterpoint here, because it's fair: in Q1.26 management also cut roughly 20% of the workforce to "align cost structure with current activity levels," and a company fully confident in an imminent V-shaped recovery does not usually cut a fifth of its staff. That tension is real. The generous reading is prudent cost discipline that protects cash while the transition plays out; the skeptical reading is that management sees the reduced run-rate persisting. The thesis does not require you to resolve that debate — it requires only that the cash floor holds while the several upside levers have time to work.

## The business is still cash-generative through the shock

Here is what makes the cash floor credible rather than a melting ice cube: Zoomd's cost structure is largely variable, so when clients pull back, the costs pull back with them.

![Still cash-generative](../../assets/theses/ZOMD.V/cash_generative.png)

The bulk of cost of revenue is publisher and traffic-source spend, which contracts directly with revenue; the fixed base is primarily Israeli salaries and a single Herzliya office. That is why 2025 — the year the shock hit — still delivered record operating cash flow of US$17.9m (up from US$7.7m in 2024) on revenue of US$61.3m, and why even Q1.26, with revenue down 62%, still produced positive operating cash flow of US$0.6m. A business that stays cash-positive at halved revenue is not going to burn through a US$22.5m cash pile; it is going to keep adding to it or hold it steady while it waits. The 20% headcount cut only reinforces that floor. This is the difference between a cheap stock that quietly bleeds and a cheap stock that can afford to be patient — Zoomd is the latter.

## Why the downside is largely on the balance sheet, and the upside is real

This is the asymmetry, laid out plainly.

![The asymmetry](../../assets/theses/ZOMD.V/asymmetry.png)

**The floor.** ~US$22.5m of net cash is ~59% of the market cap. The variable-cost model stays cash-positive even at today's depressed revenue. The 20% workforce reduction has already right-sized the cost base. Management has started a buyback, retiring stock at these depressed prices. And you're paying roughly one times cash flow for the operating business. For you to lose a lot of money permanently from here, Zoomd has to both lose its remaining business and burn its cash — and the cost model plus the cash balance make that hard.

**The upside — and it is not one bet, it is several independent ones:**

1. **The recovering customer.** One of the two paused customers is already realigning. A return toward its prior run-rate alone would materially re-rate earnings, because the market is currently valuing that revenue at close to zero. The second returning would be pure additional upside the market isn't paying for.

2. **The 2026 FIFA World Cup.** Major sporting events drive surges of user-acquisition spend from betting, media and app clients, and Zoomd has explicitly flagged the 2026 World Cup as an event-revenue opportunity concentrated in the back half of the year. It benefited from event spend before; the World Cup is a scheduled, known catalyst.

3. **The E2 sports-betting partnership.** In 2025 Zoomd formed a strategic partnership with E2, a global player in digital marketing and technology for the sports and betting industry — a direct route into iGaming and sports-betting user acquisition, one of the highest-value MarTech verticals, at exactly the moment a World Cup is coming.

4. **The scaling new-client cohort.** Diversification is not a slogan here; it is showing up in the numbers. Customers onboarded during 2025 contributed roughly 30% of Q1.26 revenue, and top-2 concentration fell from 63% (FY24) to 57% (FY25) and is still declining. New clients take 3-6 months to reach full run-rate, so the cohort already onboarded is a pipeline of revenue that hasn't fully landed yet.

![Diversification](../../assets/theses/ZOMD.V/diversification.png)

5. **Cheap tuck-in M&A for the customer books.** Management has named selective M&A as a 2026 capital-allocation priority, and the interesting angle Bohensky has described is buying small marketing/tech firms partly for their existing client relationships — a way to acquire customers and capabilities while skipping the 3-6 month organic onboarding lag and the associated business-development friction. With US$22.5m of cash and a sector full of sub-scale players, Zoomd can buy revenue cheaply rather than build it slowly.

Any one of these working pulls the operating business off its near-zero valuation. Because the enterprise value is only ~US$15-16m, a recovery toward even a fraction of prior EBITDA implies a re-rating that is a multiple of today's EV. You are not underwriting a single catalyst; you are underwriting a portfolio of them, with a cash floor underneath.

## Valuation

The valuation case is deliberately simple, which is the point. At ~US$38m market cap with ~US$22.5m of net cash, the market prices the entire operating business at ~US$15-16m — roughly 1x cash flow and ~1.5x EBITDA on 2025 figures. That is a valuation appropriate for a business the market believes is permanently and severely impaired. The evidence says otherwise: it is cash-positive even at halved revenue, one large customer is already returning, concentration is falling, and there are multiple scheduled or in-progress upside catalysts. If Zoomd stabilizes anywhere near a normalized level of profitability, the re-rating from ~1x cash flow toward even a modest single-digit multiple, on top of the cash, is a large multiple of the current enterprise value. The downside is capped by the cash and the cost model; the upside is the full recovery of an earnings stream the market is currently valuing at almost nothing.

## Risks, honestly weighted

- **Customer concentration is the core risk, and it just bit.** Two customers were 57% of FY25 revenue; the top ten were ~86%. This is exactly the risk that produced the current situation, and the second paused customer may not fully return.
- **The 20% headcount cut cuts both ways.** It protects cash, but it is also a signal that management may expect the reduced run-rate to persist rather than snap back. Take it as evidence, not just reassurance.
- **The recovery is genuinely uncertain.** Management's own language is "early signs of realignment" for one customer and "visibility remains limited" for the other. This is not a confirmed V-shape; some analysts who covered the name took losses and exited after Q1.26.
- **The new-client cohort has to keep scaling.** ~30% of Q1.26 revenue from 2025 cohorts is encouraging, but it has to grow enough to offset the lost concentration, and new clients can also churn.
- **MarTech is competitive and platform-dependent.** Apple/Google privacy changes cut both ways — they create the complexity Zoomd sells into, but they also constrain the whole ecosystem's measurability.
- **M&A execution risk.** Buying sub-scale firms for their client books is sensible in theory, but integration and retention of acquired customers is not guaranteed, and capital spent on a bad deal is capital gone.
- **Microcap illiquidity and FX.** Thin TSXV trading, a CAD quote on a USD-reporting business, and a small float mean this is a long-term, appropriately-sized ownership stake, not a trade.

## What has to be true for this to lose money permanently

A permanent loss from ~C$52m requires the operating business to keep shrinking toward zero, the ~US$22.5m cash pile to be burned or squandered on bad M&A, the recovering customer to reverse, and every upside lever — World Cup, E2, the new-client cohort — to fail simultaneously, all in a business that stayed cash-positive at 62%-down revenue and has already cut costs to match. The cost model and the cash balance make the "burn the cash" leg hard, and only one of the several upside legs needs to work for the operating business to be worth far more than the ~US$15-16m the market currently assigns it. The asymmetry — capped downside on the balance sheet, multi-lever upside — is the thesis, not a forecast that any specific catalyst lands.

## Catalysts

- **The recovering customer** returning toward prior run-rate — the single cleanest re-rating trigger, and the market is paying nothing for it.
- **2026 FIFA World Cup** event revenue landing in Q2-Q4 2026.
- **The E2 partnership** converting into sports-betting / iGaming revenue, ideally into World Cup demand.
- **The 2025 new-client cohort** continuing to scale past 30% of revenue and further cutting concentration.
- **Accretive tuck-in M&A** using the cash to buy customer books cheaply and skip the onboarding lag.
- **The buyback** retiring shares at a price where most of the market cap is cash — mechanically accretive.
- **Simple stabilization**: a couple of quarters showing the revenue base has found a floor would, by itself, challenge the market's near-permanent-impairment pricing.

*A note on sizing: Zoomd is a microcap with heavy customer concentration and a genuinely uncertain recovery. Position sizing should reflect that.*
