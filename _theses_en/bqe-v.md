## The one-paragraph version

BQE Water designs, builds and then operates water-treatment plants for the global metals mining, smelting and refining industry. The model is land-and-expand: win a feasibility study or emergency job, prove the technology, then operate the plant for the 10-20 year life of the mine on recurring tolling and service fees. Twenty-five years of proprietary process IP (SART, Selen-IX, BioSulphide) sit behind a moat that is not really about technology at all — it is that no mining executive will replace the team that designed a cyanide-handling system on First Nations land to save 10-15%, because the tail risk is a spill, a shutdown and a reputational catastrophe. 2025 was a record year — revenue C$35.5m (+107%), net income C$8.1m (+68%), a 23% net margin — and the business now runs nine long-term sites across Canada, the US and China with a pristine, net-cash balance sheet. Against ~C$90m of market cap (~C$74m EV net of cash), it trades near 8-9x owner's earnings while recurring-revenue environmental peers change hands at 15-25x. And it sits directly in the path of five structural tailwinds that all point the same way: the world is going to mine more metal, from worse ore, under stricter rules, with less water. Someone has to clean that water, and once they do, they never leave.

## The business: land, then expand forever

Headquartered in Vancouver, BQE serves metals mining, smelting and refining through two revenue streams that form a flywheel:

**Technical services (the land phase).** Feasibility studies, lab testing, pilot demonstrations, engineering design, plant commissioning. Non-recurring but strategic: it proves BQE's capability on a specific site and, critically, embeds BQE's institutional knowledge of that site's chemistry.

**Operations contracts (the expand phase).** Once BQE designs and commissions a plant, it becomes the natural operator for the life of the mine — often 10-20 years — earning recurring revenue through per-cubic-metre tolling fees, fixed monthly fees, or operations-support fees.

The flywheel is simple and brutal in its logic. A customer who hires BQE for a pilot is overwhelmingly likely to hire them for design, then construction, then operations, because switching mid-stream means handing a complex system the new provider did not design to a team the customer has never tested, in an environment where a single regulatory failure is catastrophic. Recurring revenue is now growing as a share of the total, which makes the business steadily more predictable.

The 2024-2025 transformation is real, not cosmetic. Revenue went from roughly C$12-15m of consultancy-weighted work to a C$35m+ platform. The step change came from the Eagle Mine environmental emergency in the Yukon — but the durable part is the operations book being built underneath it, which is exactly what the market is mispricing.

## Where BQE works: the footprint is the moat made visible

The clearest way to see the business is to see the sites. Each one is a multi-year annuity that competitors cannot dislodge without the customer accepting existential risk.

![Operations footprint](../../assets/theses/BQE.V/sites_footprint.png)

- **Britannia Mine (BC).** A 20-year operations-and-maintenance contract with the BC government, assumed January 13, 2026 — the largest in company history. A High-Density Sludge lime plant neutralizing acidity and removing copper and zinc from legacy underground workings before discharge into Howe Sound. Includes profit-sharing if BQE lowers historical operating costs. Transition completed smoothly in Q1 2026; contribution becomes highly visible through 2026.
- **Eagle Mine (Yukon).** The emergency that made the numbers, now transitioning to permanence. BQE designed the emergency system from scratch under crisis conditions, discharged over 1 million cubic metres of clean water safely, trained local First Nations operators, and is designing the permanent treatment system (engineering completing end of Q1 2026). The Victoria Gold receivership sale is active; whoever buys the mine inherits the water-treatment obligation, and BQE's emergency track record, permanent design and First Nations partnerships make it the overwhelming favourite for the long-term operations contract (expected H2 2026-H1 2027).
- **Nunavik Nickel (Quebec).** A three-year O&M contract via the Nuvumiut Development JV (Canadian Royalties) — an Indigenous partnership model BQE is replicating across the north.
- **Lead smelter-recycler (Eastern Canada / Quebec).** A two-year operations-support arrangement on a sulphate-removal system BQE first upgraded (new stage to <1,500 mg/L discharge), then stayed to run.
- **Minto Mine (Yukon).** Closure water management for the Yukon government since 2023.
- **Keno Hill (Yukon).** Ongoing operation (Hecla).
- **Valley Tailings / Elsa (Yukon).** A six-year water-treatment plant for the closure of a legacy tailings facility near Mayo, in partnership with the First Nation Na-Cho Nyak Dun — legacy-liability closure, a large and growing category.
- **Hudbay SART (Canada).** Canada's first SART plant, design announced February 2026 — a domestic reference case (see below).
- **China JV (JCC-BQE).** Metal recovery from cyanide and base-metal streams; seasonal, and a modest 2025 contributor, but a live operation.

And the pipeline behind the footprint: management is in active discussions for operations agreements at **four additional Canadian sites** starting in 2026, and a new aquatic toxicology laboratory came online in Q2 2026, adding specialized investigative testing to the land phase of the flywheel.

## Potential sites: how the pipeline compounds

The reason this footprint keeps expanding is that each win creates the credential for the next. Three engines drive new-site conversion:

1. **SART goes domestic.** BQE's board includes Chris Fleming, the inventor of SART (cyanide recovery in gold processing), and the company has designed or supervised a dozen SART plants globally. The Hudbay contract is Canada's *first* SART plant — which turns an internationally proven technology into a domestic reference case that BQE can sell to every Canadian gold mine fighting base-metal interference in cyanidation. That is a country-sized addressable market opening from a single reference install.

2. **Selenium regulation is a growth market with a clock on it.** Selen-IX removes selenium from mine water to sub-5-ppb levels. As selenium discharge limits tighten worldwide, the addressable market grows mechanically — and BQE has already shown (per the FY2025 commentary) that its selenium technology can take a mine from detailed engineering to compliant discharge inside 14 months, a showcase the whole industry noticed.

3. **The First Nations closure model.** Eagle, Elsa/Valley Tailings and Nunavik share a template: partner with the local Indigenous development corporation, align on clean-water production, and win the long-term operations mandate on their traditional territory. Canada has a vast inventory of legacy and closing mines requiring perpetual water treatment on Indigenous land; BQE has turned that from a regulatory headache into a repeatable partnership playbook.

## The five structural tailwinds

This is not a company betting on one contract. It is positioned in front of five forces that are independent, durable, and all pushing demand for mine water treatment in the same direction.

![Structural tailwinds](../../assets/theses/BQE.V/structural_tailwinds.png)

**1. Energy-transition metals mean more mining.** Electrification, grids, EVs and data centres are driving copper demand toward a projected shortfall of up to ~10 million tonnes per year by 2040 on current pipelines. More mines and higher throughput at existing mines both mean more contaminated water to manage. The mine water-treatment systems market was ~US$4.5bn in 2024 and is forecast to compound at roughly 9-10% into the 2030s — a rising tide independent of any single BQE contract.

**2. Falling ore grades mean more water per unit of metal.** Average copper ore grades are declining across the major producing regions. Lower grades force miners to grind finer and process far larger tonnages of ore to extract the same metal — and larger tonnages mean proportionally more contaminated water, more complex chemistry, and more selenium, sulphate and cyanide to remove. Grade decline is, quietly, a direct volume driver for BQE.

**3. Tightening ESG and discharge regulation.** Selenium, sulphate and cyanide discharge limits are ratcheting down globally, and water-discharge compliance has moved from an operational detail to a board-level, licence-to-operate risk. Every tightening of a discharge limit expands the set of mines that need exactly what BQE sells — and raises the cost of getting it wrong, which is the other half of the moat.

**4. Water scarcity turns treatment into a licence to operate.** As freshwater grows scarce in mining regions, recycling and reuse shift from optional to mandatory. Water treatment stops being a cost line to minimize and becomes infrastructure a mine cannot operate without — which is exactly the kind of demand that supports 20-year contracts.

**5. BQE's own switching costs.** The four forces above grow the market; this one determines who keeps it. In mine water treatment — particularly at sites with cyanide contamination, First Nations territory and strict oversight — switching costs approach infinity. The institutional knowledge in BQE's team cannot be transferred in a handover document. A mine might save 10-15% by switching providers; the tail risk is a cyanide spill on Indigenous land, a regulatory shutdown, litigation and reputational catastrophe. No rational executive takes that trade. Land-and-expand plus that asymmetry is why operations contracts, once won, are effectively permanent.

## The moat, in one sentence

BQE earns high returns because it combines genuine, patented process IP (a differentiated-product advantage) with switching costs that make replacing it uninsurable (a structural advantage) — and it does both in an asset-light model that needs almost no capital to grow.

## Financials and the balance sheet

FY2025 audited: revenue C$35.5m (+107%), net income C$8.1m (+68%), 23% net margin, proportional revenue (including the China JV) C$39.9m (+61%), working capital C$21.4m (+70%). Owner's earnings on a normalized run-rate sit around C$8-9m. The balance sheet carries no bank debt, an undrawn revolving facility, and cash that has climbed from C$7.9m at end-2023 to the mid-teens millions — roughly 18% of the market cap sitting in net cash. This is a business that funds its own growth and returns nothing to chance on the liability side.

A necessary honesty note on Q1 2026: revenue was C$4.8m with a small net loss (~C$1.3m). This is not deterioration — it is three things stacking in the seasonally weakest quarter: Eagle ran only one month of treatment (the successful cleanup meant the system did not need restarting until March), Britannia carried one-time transition costs from taking over the plant, and head-office and lab investment stepped up for the growth plan. Q1 is structurally BQE's weakest quarter every year (northern-hemisphere winter idles several sites; the China JV is in its dry season). The full-year 2026 setup — a full year of Britannia, the Quebec and Nunavik contracts, and Eagle's permanent transition — is what matters.

## Valuation: paying consultancy multiples for a toll operator

![Valuation vs peers](../../assets/theses/BQE.V/valuation_peers.png)

At ~C$74m EV against C$8-9m of owner's earnings, BQE trades near 8-9x — a multiple that implies the business stagnates from here. Recurring-revenue environmental-services peers typically trade 15-25x. The gap exists for three reasons, none of them about business quality:

1. **Microcap illiquidity.** ~1.30m shares outstanding and negligible daily volume make BQE structurally un-investable for institutions; price discovery is impaired. Coverage is a single analyst (Atrium Research, re-engaged for 24 months from February 2026, C$90 target).
2. **2025 read as a one-off.** The market discounts the record year as an Eagle-emergency anomaly — while ignoring the structural additions layered underneath: the 20-year Britannia contract, the Hudbay SART plant, the Quebec and Nunavik operations agreements, and Eagle's own path to a permanent multi-year contract. Even if Eagle normalizes from emergency levels, Britannia plus the new operations book more than compensates.
3. **Underappreciated switching costs.** The market prices these contracts as if they were competitively re-tendered every few years. They are not; they are effectively permanent for the reasons above.

The base case: in a probability-weighted scenario where Britannia ramps, Eagle converts to a permanent contract, and one or two pipeline projects land, owner's earnings stabilize in the C$9-12m range. A rational 12-15x on that — appropriate for asset-light, recurring, net-cash environmental infrastructure — yields a valuation well north of C$150m, before crediting the optionality in SART-goes-domestic or selenium regulation. The downside, meanwhile, is buffered by ~C$16m of net cash and a recurring operations book that does not evaporate.

## Risks, honestly weighted

- **Customer concentration.** The top four customers were 77% of 9M-2025 revenue, with Eagle a large share. Loss of Eagle without replacement would be material — mitigated by the permanent-system design position and the broadening operations book, but real.
- **Key-man risk.** CEO David Kratochvil has been central to the turnaround. In a business built on institutional knowledge and relationships, that concentration cuts both ways.
- **Eagle is not yet signed for permanence.** The receivership sale must complete and the buyer must award the long-term contract. BQE is the favourite, not the incumbent-by-right.
- **China JV variability.** JCC-BQE swings with weather and commodity prices; 2025 contribution was near historic lows.
- **Microcap illiquidity is a two-way street.** The same thin float that suppresses the multiple makes this a long-term ownership stake, not a trade — position sizing and patience matter.
- **Recent SEC registration filings.** BQE filed registration statements in late 2025 and 2026; worth watching for any equity issuance that could dilute the very tight share count (VERIFY the nature and outcome before publishing).

## What has to be true for this to lose money

A permanent capital loss from ~C$90m requires Eagle to disappear without replacement, Britannia and the new Quebec/Nunavik contracts to underdeliver, the pipeline to stall, and the five structural tailwinds to reverse — all against a net-cash balance sheet and a recurring operations book with 10-20 year contract lives and switching costs that make displacement uninsurable. The evidence points the other way on each count: the operations book is growing, the tailwinds are strengthening, and the moat is widening as regulation tightens. Asymmetry, not a growth forecast, is the thesis.
