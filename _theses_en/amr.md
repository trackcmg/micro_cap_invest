## The one-paragraph version

Alpha is the largest pure-play metallurgical coal producer in the United States — it sells the coking coal that becomes the coke that reduces iron ore to iron inside a blast furnace, a process that still makes roughly 70% of the world's steel and for which there is no scaled substitute today. This is a violently cyclical commodity, and AMR has enormous operating leverage to it: at the 2022 peak the company earned about $1.45bn of net income and $1.3bn of free cash flow — in one year, against a market capitalization of roughly $2.2bn today. It carries no bank debt, holds several hundred million in cash, and has spent the down-cycle relentlessly cannibalizing its own shares, retiring about 31% of the company since 2022. The structural setup is the interesting part: years of ESG-driven capital starvation are shrinking the met-coal supply base precisely as India builds out blast-furnace steelmaking, and because a new mine takes the better part of a decade to permit and ramp, supply cannot answer a demand spike quickly. You do not need to forecast the next spike. You need a fortress balance sheet that survives the trough and a management that shrinks the share count while waiting — which is exactly what two very different value investors, Mohnish Pabrai and AMR director Kenneth Courtis, are betting on as they accumulate at these levels.

## Why metallurgical coal is not "just coal"

The single most important distinction in this thesis is the one the market blurs: metallurgical (coking) coal and thermal coal are different products with different destinies. Thermal coal is burned for electricity and is in structural, permanent decline as renewables displace it. Metallurgical coal is a chemical reactant in steelmaking, and its demand is coupled to steel, not to power.

Here is the chemistry, because it is the moat. Steel is an alloy of iron and carbon; iron exists in the earth's crust only as iron oxide, which must be chemically reduced to metallic iron. In the dominant blast-furnace / basic-oxygen-furnace route — roughly 70% of world steel — that reduction is done by coke, which is almost-pure carbon made by baking metallurgical coal at ~1,000-1,100°C in an oxygen-free oven for 12-36 hours. The coke does three jobs at once inside the furnace: it is the fuel, it is the chemical reducing agent that strips oxygen from the ore, and it is the permeable physical structure that holds up the ore burden and lets gas and molten metal pass. No other input does all three at industrial scale. Scrap-fed electric arc furnaces (EAF) are growing and matter at the margin, but they cannot replace primary steelmaking: you cannot build the world's incremental steel out of recycled steel alone, and high-quality primary steel still needs the blast-furnace route. The steel industry consumes roughly three-quarters of all metallurgical coal, and in a recent year used on the order of 750 million tonnes of it. Steel is embodied everywhere — buildings, grids, cars, railways, wind turbines, defence — and the energy transition itself is steel-intensive. Demand for the end product is not going away.

The bear case, which deserves stating plainly: the IEA projects global met-coal demand declining gradually this decade (from ~1,114 Mt toward ~1,061 Mt by 2030) as EAF share rises and Chinese steel plateaus. That is real and I am not dismissing it. But two things blunt it. First, a gentle demand glide-path matters far less than the supply response when the supply base is being starved faster than demand fades — see below. Second, the IEA itself flags India as the offsetting force: India's blast-furnace build-out and thin domestic coking-coal endowment make it a growing structural importer, exactly the demand leg the consensus keeps underestimating.

## The structural squeeze: essential product, shrinking supply, decade-long response lag

This is the core of the thesis, and it is a supply story more than a demand story.

![Structural thesis](../../assets/theses/AMR/structural_thesis.png)

**ESG has starved the supply of new mines.** For most of the last decade, capital for new metallurgical coal projects has been withdrawn on ESG grounds — banks won't lend, funds won't hold, insurers won't cover. Industry executives are blunt about the consequence. Core Natural Resources' CEO described met-coal output as "constrained by years of underinvestment, ongoing degradation and depletion of the global reserve base, as well as continuing regulatory pressure." That is not a cyclical shortage; it is a structural erosion of the productive base. The same ESG pressure that makes the sector uninvestable for many is precisely what creates the scarcity — the market is defunding future supply of a product the world still needs.

**A new mine takes the better part of a decade.** Metallurgical coal is not a spigot. Permitting, development, and ramping a greenfield mine runs roughly 5-10 years. This is why the supply side cannot respond to a price spike: by the time high prices would justify new capacity, the cycle has often turned again. The response lag is structural, and it is what makes met-coal prices move so violently — small shifts in supply or demand have nowhere to go but price.

**Depletion is silent but relentless.** Existing mines deplete; reserves degrade; the best seams get mined first. Without sustained reinvestment, the aggregate supply base ratchets down year after year even before any new demand shock.

Put the three together — an essential, hard-to-substitute product; a supply base being actively starved and depleted; and a decade-long lag before supply can respond — and you have the classic precondition for violent, repeated price spikes. You are not betting on a single catalyst. You are betting on a structural mismatch that resolves, again and again, through the price of the commodity.

## The cyclicality, and AMR's leverage to it

Met coal is one of the most cyclical commodities there is. The Platts High Vol A US East Coast benchmark hit roughly $300/tonne at its October 2023 two-year high and fell to about $172/tonne by May 2025 — a 43% drop in eighteen months — and that is a normal amplitude for this market, not an outlier.

![Coal price cycle](../../assets/theses/AMR/coal_price_cycle.png)

AMR is a high-operating-leverage way to own that cycle. Its costs are relatively fixed; its realized price is not; so the swing in the coal price drops through to cash flow with amplification. The whole point of owning the largest, best-capitalized producer is that this leverage cuts in your favour at the top and does not kill you at the bottom.

## The peak-year economics against today's price

This is the number that reframes the opportunity. Look at what AMR did across one full cycle:

![Peak earnings vs market cap](../../assets/theses/AMR/peak_vs_marketcap.png)

In 2022 — the peak — AMR generated revenue of about $4.1bn, net income of roughly $1.45bn, and free cash flow of about $1.3bn. In one year. The entire company today is worth about $2.2bn. A single peak year produced free cash flow equal to well over half the current market capitalization, and net income equal to roughly two-thirds of it.

The cycle then did what cycles do. Revenue fell to $3.47bn (2023), $2.96bn (2024) and $2.13bn (2025); free cash flow stepped down from $1.3bn (2022) to $575m (2023) to $349m (2024) and went slightly negative in 2025; the trough year produced adjusted EBITDA of only $121.9m and a small net loss. Q4 2025 was a ~$17m net loss. This is a trough, and the thesis does not pretend otherwise — it depends on it. The question is never what AMR earns at the bottom; it is what it earns across a cycle, and whether it can survive the bottom in a shape to capture the next top. On both counts the balance sheet is the answer.

## The fortress balance sheet and the buyback machine

In the coal industry, permanent capital impairment comes from two things: too much leverage, and operational disruption during a downturn. AMR has deliberately engineered both risks down. It runs with essentially no bank debt (total debt-to-equity around 0.3%) and holds several hundred million dollars of cash — a fortress designed to stay profitable in strong markets and viable in weak ones. That conservatism is the entire reason a cyclical, out-of-favour commodity producer can be a rational long-term holding rather than a wipeout risk: when weaker, levered peers are forced to cut and sell at the bottom, AMR simply waits.

And while it waits, it eats itself — in the best possible way.

![Buyback cannibal](../../assets/theses/AMR/buyback_cannibal.png)

Since March 2022, AMR has repurchased roughly 6.9 million shares for about $1.1bn, reducing basic shares outstanding by approximately 31%. It suspended the dividend to prioritize buybacks and has done so through the down-cycle, when the shares are cheapest — the correct time to retire stock. A further ~$1.5bn repurchase authorization remains unused. This is the "cannibal" pattern value investors prize: a company with strong through-cycle cash generation converting that cash into a permanently smaller share count, so that every future peak is divided across far fewer shares. If met coal simply cycles back toward a normal-to-high price with the share count already down a third, the per-share earnings power is dramatically higher than it was at the last peak. The buyback is not financial engineering; it is the mechanism that turns cyclical cash into compounding per-share value.

## The smart money: two very different investors, buying the same trough

When an insider who understands the assets and an outside value investor who understands the demand independently accumulate the same out-of-favour name at the bottom, it is worth paying attention.

![Aligned buyers](../../assets/theses/AMR/aligned_buyers.png)

**Mohnish Pabrai** — managing partner of Pabrai Investment Funds and Dhandho Funds, an avowed Buffett/Munger disciple and author of *The Dhandho Investor* — made a remarkable concentration bet on met coal. At one point he sold out of essentially all his other US positions (Micron, Brookfield, Seritage and more) and concentrated his entire US 13F into metallurgical coal producers: AMR, Warrior Met Coal (HCC) and Core/CONSOL. AMR was around 38% of that US book at the end of 2024. His approach is textbook Dhandho — "heads I win, tails I don't lose much," low downside from the fortress balance sheet, high upside from the cycle and the buybacks. His average cost has been reported around $183/share; the pattern of averaging down across the coal names through the trough is exactly what a value investor does when conviction is high and price is falling.

**Kenneth Courtis** is the insider, and his profile is unusual. He is an AMR director, the former vice-chairman and managing director of Goldman Sachs (Asia), and former chief economist and investment strategist at Deutsche Bank Asia — a genuine authority on Asian economies and commodity cycles, and chairman of Starfort Investment Holdings. This matters because the demand leg of the met-coal thesis is an Asia story (China plateauing, India rising), and Courtis has spent his career reading exactly those forces. He has been buying AMR aggressively in the open market — over $4.3 million in recent months, including about $2.0 million on June 12, 2026 at roughly $200-201/share — and doing so directly into a Wall Street "Reduce" consensus. An insider with his macro pedigree putting personal capital in at the trough, against the sell-side, is one of the higher-quality signals available.

Two investors, opposite vantage points — one reads the balance sheet and the buyback, the other reads Asian steel demand and the cycle — converging on the same conclusion at the same price.

## Valuation: what you're paying for

At ~$2.2bn, you are buying the largest US pure-play met-coal producer, debt-free, with a fortress balance sheet, at the bottom of its cycle, with about a third of the shares already retired and a further large authorization outstanding. On trough 2025 numbers the trailing multiple looks unremarkable — that is what a trough does. The relevant frame is through-cycle: a business that can earn $1.3bn of free cash flow in a peak year, now worth $2.2bn in total, with a share count down 31%, does not require heroic assumptions to be cheap. It requires only that the cycle behaves like a cycle. If met coal spends any meaningful time near normal-to-high prices over the next few years, the per-share cash generation against today's price is highly asymmetric. The downside is genuinely buffered — no debt, real cash, lowest-risk operating posture — and the upside is the full operating leverage of the largest producer, divided across a shrinking share base.

This is a Dhandho setup in the literal sense Pabrai means it: the balance sheet caps the downside, and the cycle plus the buybacks provide the upside.

## Risks, honestly weighted

- **It is a commodity cyclical, and this is the trough.** 2025 was a small loss; Q4 2025 lost ~$17m; FCF is negative right now. If the down-cycle is long and deep, the wait is painful and the buyback slows. You are underwriting the trough, not escaping it.
- **The demand glide-path is real.** The IEA does project gradual met-coal demand decline to 2030 on rising EAF share and Chinese steel maturity. The India offset and the supply-starvation dynamic are the counter, but the bear case is not frivolous.
- **China is the swing.** Chinese steel and property weakness has driven the price down before and can again; Chinese import behaviour dominates the seaborne market.
- **Execution and operational risk.** Underground Appalachian mining carries safety, geological and disruption risk (the sector has seen mine fires and outages); AMR's Kingston Wildcat greenfield ramp must execute.
- **No dividend today.** Capital return is entirely via buyback; income investors get nothing now, and the buyback is discretionary.
- **Wall Street disagrees.** The sell-side consensus is "Reduce" with price targets barely above the current price. That is either the opportunity or the warning, depending on your time horizon — but it means no near-term analyst tailwind.
- **Policy and trade.** Tariffs, trade policy and export-market access materially affect a producer that ships a large share of output abroad.

## What has to be true for this to lose money permanently

A permanent loss from ~$2.2bn requires met coal to stay near trough prices for years, the fortress balance sheet to erode despite no debt and real cash, the buyback to stop compounding per-share value, and India's structural import growth plus the ESG-driven supply starvation to both fail to tighten the market across an entire cycle — all in a business selling an input that primary steelmaking cannot currently replace. Cycles have not historically behaved that way, and a debt-free producer retiring a third of its shares at the bottom is engineered precisely to survive the wait. The asymmetry — capped downside from the balance sheet, cyclical-plus-buyback upside — is the thesis, not a price forecast.

## Catalysts

- **Any met-coal price recovery** dropping through AMR's operating leverage into cash flow and reigniting the buyback at a lower share count.
- **India import growth** becoming undeniable in the seaborne trade data, validating the demand leg the consensus underweights.
- **Continued supply attrition** — mine closures, capex cuts and depletion across a capital-starved industry tightening the market from the supply side.
- **The buyback itself**: ~$1.5bn of authorization against a ~$2.2bn company means continued repurchases are enormously accretive per share at these prices.
- **Insider and guru accumulation** continuing to signal conviction against a bearish sell-side.
- **Kingston Wildcat** ramping to add low-vol met volume into an eventual price recovery.
